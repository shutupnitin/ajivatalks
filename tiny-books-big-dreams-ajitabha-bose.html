Tiny Books, Big Dreams — A Conversation with Ajitabha Bose

By Ajiva Talks • Author Interviews • — min read

Ajitabha Bose is a bestselling author, filmmaker and publisher known for compact storytelling and creative entrepreneurship. Hailing from Jamshedpur and now based in Delhi, he introduced the pocketbook concept to India and holds a national record for publishing the smallest love storybook. With nine books and over a million readers, his work spans fiction, filmmaking and publishing.

Ajitabha Bose is a celebrated Indian bestselling author, filmmaker, and publisher who has made a notable mark across literature and media. He holds a national record for publishing the smallest love storybook in India. Originally from Jamshedpur, he studied at Gulmohur High School and later at the Institute of Management Studies, Ghaziabad. Today he is based in Delhi and runs creative and publishing ventures including Ajitabha Publishers and BDL Studios.

Ajitabha introduced pocketbooks to the Indian market — a compact storytelling format aimed at readers who prefer shorter reads. He has authored nine books including It’s A Love Story (2013), The Pocket Love Story (2015), In Love with Shah Rukh Khan (2016), The Bestseller (2017), It’s My Love Story (2018), Until We Meet Again (2019), Love in Jamshedpur (2020), and The YouTube Stars of India (2021). His recent work Ye Baarish, Ishq aur Tum… (2021) continues his focus on love and contemporary culture. His authorized profile of Indian digital creators in The YouTube Stars of India includes CarryMinati, Amit Bhadana, Ashish Chanchlani, Technical Guruji, Prajakta Koli and others.

1. When did you first realize you wanted to be a writer?
Ajitabha Bose: I grew up in a Bengali home where stories and Tagore’s poems were always part of life. I wrote essays and poems in school and contributed to the school magazine. The first time I thought, “Yes, I can write a book” was in college around 2010 — and in 2013 I finally published my first book.
2. Why choose to write small books?
Ajitabha Bose: I got the idea while traveling to Jamshedpur for Dussehra. On the train I met a girl named Neha who asked how I could read a “fat book.” She suggested people might prefer shorter books. That stuck with me. Back in Delhi I had a short story ready but publishers turned it down. A week or ten days later I thought — why not a pocket book? We have pocket diaries and pocket dictionaries; why not pocket books? That was the start of the pocket-book idea.
3. Where do you get ideas for your books?
Ajitabha Bose: I write fiction, but fiction draws from life. Everyone has a story — a CEO, a watchman — you only have to look and listen. I find inspiration in people and everyday moments, and then reshape them into stories.
4. When did you write your first book and how old were you?
Ajitabha Bose: I wrote my first book in 2013 during my third year of college. I was 21. I never imagined I would become a bestselling author — it happened gradually.
5. What does your family think of your writing?
Ajitabha Bose: My family has always supported me. My grandfather was a theatre artist and my father still performs and directs plays across India. They were proud when I started writing and remain proud of the work I do in books and film.
6. What surprised you most about publishing?
Ajitabha Bose: I was surprised — and a little shocked — to learn that writing a good book doesn’t guarantee sales. Marketing matters a lot. A strong book without a marketing plan or budget can struggle. That lesson changed how I approach publishing and promotion.
7. In childhood, what did you want to be when you grew up?
Ajitabha Bose: When I was very young I wanted to be a software engineer — I was good with computers. Later, inspired by my father and theatre, I wanted to make films. Writing wasn’t my first plan, but it came to me and I embraced it. I’m glad it did.
