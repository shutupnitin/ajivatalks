Stories, Diaspora & Becoming — A Conversation with Zara Raheem

By Ajiva Talks • Author Interviews • — min read

Zara Raheem writes with compassion and clarity about identity, belonging, and the pressures faced by first- and second-generation immigrants. Her debut novel The Marriage Clock earned wide acclaim; her work continues to illuminate the South Asian diaspora with warmth and honesty.

Zara Raheem holds an MFA in Creative Writing from California State University, Long Beach. She received the James I. Murashige Jr. Memorial Award in Fiction and was named a Harriet Williams Emerging Writer in 2019. Her debut novel, The Marriage Clock, appeared on numerous "must-read" lists and has been translated into Italian and Portuguese. Her second novel, The Retreat, was released in April 2023. Zara teaches English and creative writing in Southern California and is developing a short story collection that explores the South Asian diaspora and Muslim-American experience.

1. Can you share your journey as a writer and what inspired you to pursue writing as a career?
Zara Raheem: Ever since I was young, I’ve loved creating and exercising my imagination through storytelling. Writing began as a hobby, but in my mid-twenties I found the courage to pursue it professionally. Teaching and community have also sustained my practice as a writer.
2. Your debut novel The Marriage Clock received critical acclaim. What inspired the story, and how did you approach the writing process?
Zara Raheem: The book was initially inspired by my own experience with the arranged-marriage process. I used writing to make sense of those moments. As I developed the story, it became its own thing — while many events and characters are fictional, the emotional journey Leila goes through is rooted in my own life.
3. The novel explores themes of cultural identity, tradition, and societal expectations. How important is it for you to include these themes, and what message did you hope to convey?
Zara Raheem: I grappled with these issues myself, so I wanted to share a story that others might see themselves in. My hope is to shed light on pressures around marriage and family in our communities and to encourage readers — especially young women — to shape their own paths to happiness, even when that challenges cultural expectations.
4. How do you approach character development? Any techniques for creating authentic, relatable characters?
Zara Raheem: I love seeing characters transform. I focus not only on the arc of change but on how that transformation will resonate emotionally with readers. I think about small, concrete choices that reveal who a character is and how they change over time.
5. The Marriage Clock addresses ageism, colorism and gender biases. Why is it important to explore these topics in fiction?
Zara Raheem: These are topics we often don’t discuss openly in South Asian communities. Fiction can create a space to examine them critically; I hope books like The Marriage Clock encourage conversations and reflection about normalized but harmful behaviors.
6. What advice would you give aspiring writers?
Zara Raheem: Keep writing. Trust yourself. Keep sharing the stories that matter to you — persistence and belief in your work are essential.
7. Any themes you plan to explore in upcoming projects?
Zara Raheem: I’m interested in themes that speak to me and readers — I’m working on another novel and a short story collection focused on diaspora and the Muslim-American experience, but it’s still early. I’m excited to share more soon.
