Author Interview

Puneet Sikka — Take No. 2020

By Ajiva Talks • Fiction • — min read

Puneet Sikka speaks about her debut novel Take No. 2020, an unfiltered look at ambition, identity, humour and grief in Bollywood — set against the chaos of a year that changed everything.

Quick facts

Debut: Take No. 2020

Genres: Literary fiction

Background: Media, acting, writing

Education: LSR, SIMC Pune

1. What inspired you to write Take No. 2020?
Puneet Sikka: I felt compelled to tell a story that had been brewing for a while, especially after moving to Bombay and auditioning as an actor. By 2020, reality forced me to reimagine everything. These characters represented thousands of aspirants navigating Bollywood, and intersecting their lives reflected the isolation and intensity of the pandemic year.
2. How much of the book is drawn from real-life experiences?
Puneet Sikka: Most creative work eventually draws from life. Take No. 2020 is largely auto-fictional, inspired by my own experiences and those of actor friends, acquaintances, and stories absorbed from the world around me.
3. How did you develop the characters and their arcs?
Puneet Sikka: The characters wrote their own arcs through me. Much of 2020 went into scribbling plot points. The hardest part was empathising with characters I didn’t agree with — but they had to feel like heroes in their own minds.
4. Identity plays a central role in the novel. Why?
Puneet Sikka: Actors constantly navigate multiple identities, which mirrors broader human struggles of belonging and self-definition. The characters act as mirrors to societal issues and collective human unconscious.
5. How did you balance humour and grief?
Puneet Sikka: I viewed the story as “one person’s tragedy is another’s comedy.” I allowed characters to find humour or grief organically, leaving interpretation open to the reader.
6. Why include themes like #MeToo and the casting couch?
Puneet Sikka: These themes are unfortunately relevant to showbiz. I didn’t intend to moralise but was drawn to human endurance and growth in difficult circumstances.
7. Can you explain the “story within a story” structure?
Puneet Sikka: I discovered this structure only after finishing the book. It suited the film-based setting perfectly — and saying more would mean spoilers.
8. How does Bollywood as a setting intensify the narrative?
Puneet Sikka: Bollywood’s low entry barrier and brutal survival curve already create conflict. Add 2020 to that, and it became a Hunger Games–like situation.
9. What was your research process like?
Puneet Sikka: Much came from lived experience. For specialised aspects, I consulted experts and had intentional conversations with industry professionals.
10. Do the characters find redemption?
Puneet Sikka: Redemption itself can be the resolution. Life exists in the in-between, and so does this story — open to interpretation.
