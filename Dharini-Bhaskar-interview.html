Dharini Bhaskar — Like Being Alive Twice: Love, Privilege & Possibility

By Ajiva Talks
•
Fiction / Interviews
•
— min read

Dharini Bhaskar’s Like Being Alive Twice alternates reality and imagined possibilities to investigate love, privilege and political rupture. We spoke about the novel’s origin, its split narrative, and why stories must hold both fact and what-ifs.

Quick facts

Book: Like Being Alive Twice

Themes: Love, privilege, political urgency, alternate possibilities

Role: Novelist

Fiction, narrative experiment, social conscience

1. What inspired you to write "Like Being Alive Twice"? Was there a specific event or experience that sparked the idea for this story?
Dharini Bhaskar: For me, all stories begin with a word—a word I can wed myself to. For this novel, the word is poppy. I love the sound and what it signifies—John McCrae's poem about poppies in Flanders Fields: delicate red flowers growing against rivers of blood. That contrast — possibility set against violence — held a rich promise and around it the story grew.
2. The novel explores love, privilege and political turmoil. Why choose these themes and what makes them urgent now?
Dharini Bhaskar: The story chose them. We can’t ignore the world we live in when we write. As Marwan Makhoul and Neruda remind us, one cannot pretend the world isn’t political when blood fills the streets. These themes reflect the reality many live in and the book tries to hold that urgency rather than look away.
3. You alternate between actual events and potential outcomes. What motivated that structure and what effect did you want?
Dharini Bhaskar: At some level we all yearn to know whether changing a moment changes everything. A split narrative allows each possibility to be followed and witnessed for what it harbours. The structure felt like an honest way to explore choice, chance and consequence.
4. Poppy and Tariq’s love happens in a nation on the brink. Why place their relationship in such a fraught environment?
Dharini Bhaskar: I don’t see a world that is gentle right now—glaciers melt, people rage, and violence is present. If I want writing to mirror what is or speak of tomorrow, love must take root in dark, merciless ground. The fragility and resilience of love are therefore shown most starkly against that backdrop.
5. How did you approach portraying privilege through Poppy and Tariq’s relationship?
Dharini Bhaskar: Focusing on privilege was deliberate. Privilege often brings an illusion of safety and immunity—the belief that the outside world cannot intrude on one’s comfortable sphere. I wanted to test that assumption through Poppy: would her birthright of safety hold against a rampaging state? The moral question—does privilege protect or isolate?—is central to the book.
6. How did you weave fact and possibility together—what was your process?
Dharini Bhaskar: I imagined moments and then reimagined them—like drawing in charcoal: sketch, blow it off, and sketch again on the same sheet. That iterative, layered process let me explore multiple trajectories without losing the book’s emotional core.
7. The novel pursues meaning in turbulent times. What, if anything, do you hope readers take away?
Dharini Bhaskar: I don’t write to hand readers messages. I hope readers find a sentence they can hold, a word they carry in their pockets, and perhaps rediscover poetry. Each reader’s conversation with a book is private and transformative; I trust those conversations to happen.
8. What challenges did you face writing about such sensitive and urgent topics?
Dharini Bhaskar: My main challenge has been time—especially with small children who need constant care. Carving short snatches of time to world-build and word-hunt has been the toughest part. Creatively, the challenge is finding those pockets where a sentence can sing and grow.
9. The book is described as politically urgent and stylistically intrepid. How did you balance storytelling with social concerns?
Dharini Bhaskar: I let the story lead. It’s dangerous to let topical issues steer fiction at the cost of character and voice. The political themes emerge naturally from the characters’ lives—Poppy, Tariq, Fatima, Mariam, Mitali remain the heart of the book; the politics extend from them, not the other way around.
10. Finally, what would you like readers to remember after finishing "Like Being Alive Twice"?
Dharini Bhaskar: I hope they find a sentence to hold or a word to carry. I hope they are reminded of poetry. Beyond that, reading is a pact between book and reader—an active, transformative act—and I hope the book becomes part of that private, vital conversation.
