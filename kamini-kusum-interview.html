Kamini Kusum — Dare to Shine, Women Changemakers & Research-Driven Storytelling

By Ajiva Talks
•
Profiles / Women's Leadership
•
— min read

Kamini Kusum’s new book Dare to Shine: Inspiring Stories of 20 Women Changemakers showcases powerful biographical sketches of women who broke barriers — from Droupadi Murmu to P.V. Sindhu. In this interview she discusses research, selection, narrative technique and the driving message behind the collection.

Quick facts

Book: Dare to Shine — 20 Women Changemakers

Other works: Difficult Girls series, Gunpoint Groom, Honey & the Moon

Interests: Yoga, food, cinema

1. Can you tell us about the inspiration behind your latest book, "Dare to Shine: Inspiring Stories of 20 Women Changemakers"? What prompted you to embark on this project?
Kamini Kusum: Dare to Shine is my 7th paperback apart from 32 digital books I’ve written across series. Women have always held prominent roles in my fiction and non-fiction. The idea for this book came from my publisher — when we discussed the concept I was excited. Writing about strong women is close to my heart. This was my first non-fiction project in this vein and it demanded deep primary and secondary research. I don’t write without giving full effort to research the subject, characters, locations and the arc of the narrative.
2. The book features twenty remarkable women from diverse fields. How did you go about selecting these women, and what criteria did you consider?
Kamini Kusum: I kept several criteria in mind. The women had to come from diverse fields, span across time (history and present), and have truly inspiring lives. They needed to have broken glass ceilings or defied stereotypes. I also wanted to include women who have achieved great things but remain under-recognized. The purpose was to bring a mix of known and lesser-known changemakers into the spotlight.
3. Could you share some of the most memorable or impactful stories from the book? What sets these women apart?
Kamini Kusum: I was deeply moved by President Droupadi Murmu’s story — the first tribal person and second woman to hold the office. Her early life, educational struggles and personal tragedies were immense, yet she persisted. Another striking story is Neera Arya — a spy in the Indian National Army — whose sacrifices and resilience were harrowing and inspiring. Each woman in the book has a distinct bravery and a story that deserves wider recognition.
4. What challenges did you encounter while researching and presenting these women's lives authentically?
Kamini Kusum: Some subjects, like Neera Arya and Sarla Thukral, had limited accessible content. Finding facts meant combing archives and visiting libraries. I wanted the book to convey not just facts but emotions, pains and struggles — elements that truly impact readers and inspire them. Gathering authentic material required extra effort.
5. Across these stories, what common traits did you notice and how can these narratives inspire others?
Kamini Kusum: Many of these women came from patriarchal societies and still pursued paths considered “men’s domains”: science, mountaineering, aviation, politics, even detective work. Their common trait is resilience — they refused to buckle under pressure and pursued their dreams with determination. Their stories show readers that with hard work and courage, nothing is impossible.
6. How did your background in fiction influence the way you told these non-fiction lives?
Kamini Kusum: My fiction writing taught me to reveal suspense and emotion gradually, and I applied similar storytelling techniques to non-fiction. Non-fiction can be more compelling when it doesn’t read like a list of facts — by revealing emotions and challenges bit by bit, the chapters feel like real-life thrillers and keep readers engaged.
7. Given your corporate and management background, how do these stories resonate with professionals and leaders?
Kamini Kusum: My management and HR experience helped me understand people’s motivations and psychology. That perspective helps me present characters and situations in a way professionals can relate to. Leaders can draw lessons on resilience, empathy, and the human side of decision-making from these narratives.
8. The title "Dare to Shine" carries a strong message. What do you hope young women and girls take away?
Kamini Kusum: I want girls with dreams in their eyes to know there’s nothing they can’t accomplish. With determination, hard work and passion, women can succeed in any field. Fortune favors the brave.
9. Many featured women have multifaceted lives. How did you balance portraying their identities with focusing on achievements?
Kamini Kusum: Balance is about strategic writing. Whether fiction or non-fiction, I ensure emotions and facts are paced so readers don’t get fatigued. Years of writing across genres helped me shape each chapter so it highlights achievements while respecting the subject’s full humanity.
10. What impact do you envision "Dare to Shine" having on conversations about women’s empowerment and leadership?
Kamini Kusum: This book is ambitious and the result of extensive research and interviews. Its aims are multifold: to spotlight inspiring stories, to preserve lesser-known facts, and to give recognition where due. I hope it fuels conversations about courage, leadership, and the many ways women have contributed to society. Ultimately, it’s about celebrating courage and a ‘come-what-may, I will do it’ spirit.
