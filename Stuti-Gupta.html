Stuti Gupta — Mythology, Children’s Books & Storytelling

By Ajiva Talks
•
Children’s Literature / Editorial
•
— min read

Stuti Gupta — Chief Editor at Srishti Publishers and author of bestselling children’s books Magical Mythology and Vahana — is a dynamic voice shaping India’s young readers. In this candid conversation, she shares her journey, her love for storytelling, and how motherhood transformed her into an author.

Quick Facts

Role: Chief Editor, Srishti Publishers

Books: Magical Mythology, Vahana

Specialization: Children’s Literature & Mythology

Experience: 18+ years

Donate

1. Can you tell us about your journey in the publishing industry and how you found your passion for children's literature?
Stuti Gupta: I have been a part of the publishing industry since 2006, in various capacities, and have been actively involved in making books for close to 18 years now. Starting off as a content creator and growing in editorial duties, I now spearhead the editorial department at Srishti Publishers.

I never imagined I would write myself — editors are often their own toughest critics! But when my two-year-old demanded a new story at every meal, motherhood turned me into a storyteller. That is how I entered the magical world of children’s writing.
2. Your book “Vahana” has gained popularity. What inspired you to retell mythological tales for children?
Stuti Gupta: When I went looking for well-researched Indian stories for toddlers, I found mostly Western fairy tales. That shocked me. With my academic background in scriptures, I revisited my research papers to find stories that were informative, entertaining, and rooted in India.

To make them child-friendly, I included activities and wrote in a tone similar to how I narrate stories to my own son.
3. How do you merge academic knowledge with storytelling for young minds?
Stuti Gupta: It’s tough! I know more than I should include, but children need simplicity. I write assuming the reader knows nothing. That builds clarity and engagement.
4. What motivated you to compile “Popular Indian Fairy Tales”?
Stuti Gupta: I wanted children to know India’s rich storytelling heritage — epics, bards, vrat-kathas, folklore. We have incredible stories, and children deserve to access them.
5. Your books include illustrations and activities. How do these enhance reading?
Stuti Gupta: Children have short attention spans. Visuals and activities keep them engaged and make learning joyful. Illustrator Ishan Trivedi brought my stories to life beautifully.
6. Has being Chief Editor influenced your writing?
Stuti Gupta: Absolutely! The advantage is real feedback from readers and parents. The downside is that I over-critique my own writing. I rewrite multiple times before calling it final.
7. You test stories on your five-year-old. How useful is that?
Stuti Gupta: Very! Children are honest critics. Their reactions help me understand pacing, difficulty level, and what excites them.
8. How do you balance preserving traditional tales with making them modern?
Stuti Gupta: Each story subtly addresses topics like gender, safety, emotions, equality, and social behaviour — without lecturing. Stories bridge generations.
9. How has children’s literature evolved?
Stuti Gupta: It’s at its experimental peak — from travel diaries to mythology to life-skills books. With the internet, half-baked knowledge won’t work; deep research is essential.
10. How important is joy in learning, and how do you incorporate it?
Stuti Gupta: Writing comes from reading. Children learn only when they enjoy the process. Stories have the power to change lives — that’s the foundation of my work.
