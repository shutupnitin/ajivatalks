Muneeta Aneja — Journalism, Rural Reporting & Chhattisgarh

By Ajiva Talks
•
Development Journalism / Interviews
•
— min read

Muneeta Aneja is a gold-medalist journalist and Editor-in-Chief of The New Age. Her work centers on development and rural journalism; she has spent months documenting tribal rituals, archaeology and living cultures across Chhattisgarh for her forthcoming book Chhattisgarh — A Land of Infinite Treasures. Below is an extended conversation about her work, methods and vision.

Quick facts

Role: Editor-in-Chief, The New Age

Focus: Rural & development journalism

Book: Chhattisgarh — A Land of Infinite Treasures (upcoming)

Donate

1. Can you tell us about your experience in the field of journalism and editorial work, particularly leading a publication?
Muneeta Aneja: I have been in Journalism and Mass Communication for more than six years, working in diverse editorial roles. I began with internships at Dainik Bhaskar (Bhopal) and IBC 24 (Chhattisgarh), and by 2017–18 I contributed blog posts to national and international outlets and headed editorial fashion segments for e-magazines. Over time I focused on PR content that allowed me creative control and authenticity.

By 2019 I started contributing to the Times of India across supplements and special editions — writing roughly 200 articles on civic issues, cybercrime awareness, heritage, mental health, and more. In 2019 I also served as PR Executive for DPR Samvaad (CMO, Chhattisgarh) handling press releases, speeches, crisis communications, and social media for 18 months. After that I returned to freelancing and editorial work, including CSR and coffee-table projects.

I launched The New Age by Muneeta Aneja to provide credible, simplified reporting. Within two and a half years we reached readers in 84 countries. Our editorial approach prioritised verified sources and in-depth explainers rather than sensationalism. By late 2022 I committed to development journalism, documenting Chhattisgarh to highlight rural heritage, boost micro-tourism, and create sustainable economic opportunities for villages. I spent long stretches on the ground — crossing mountains and riverbeds, interviewing locals, and studying archaeology, mythology, and anthropology — to build an integrated view for the book.
2. What sets The New Age apart and what is your vision for the publication?
Muneeta Aneja: The New Age functions as a credible news portal and e-magazine. As Editor-in-Chief I ensured that PR, advertising, or bias does not taint our articles. We simplify geopolitics and complex topics into short, digestible explainers while maintaining rigorous sourcing. We balance heavy investigative pieces with lifestyle and travel features to keep the site accessible and varied. My vision is to sustain credibility, prioritise verified reporting, and promote issues—like mental health and rural development—that are often overlooked.
3. How do you stay up-to-date and keep The New Age relevant?
Muneeta Aneja: I stay connected to credible news agencies, official DPR releases, and verified government data. We focus on topics that retain long-term relevance (e.g., geopolitics, development) and create evergreen content such as travelogues and poems. Using analytics, we tailor content to reader interests while ensuring accuracy and depth.
4. What strategies maintain credibility and integrity at The New Age?
Muneeta Aneja: I prioritise rigorous research and fact-checking, separate PR from journalism, and avoid sensationalism. We present findings with balanced language and allow readers to form their own conclusions. Accuracy is non-negotiable, and editorial decisions are driven by evidence, not clicks.
5. How do you balance in-depth stories with diverse reader interests?
Muneeta Aneja: We analysed audience behaviour and found two main cohorts: readers who love longform investigatory pieces and readers who prefer lifestyle/fashion/travel. We publish both — keeping flagship investigative reporting as the backbone while sprinkling in accessible features to maintain broad appeal.
6. How do you use digital platforms and emerging tech to grow reach?
Muneeta Aneja: We leveraged LinkedIn, Twitter and WordPress, and used targeted hashtags and monthly newsletters. Analytics guided content strategy and helped us find the right audiences. Newsletters remain a core channel for loyal readers.
7. How do you combat bias, fake news and disinformation?
Muneeta Aneja: Our policy is to rely on trusted data sources (NCRB, official releases) and cross-check facts multiple times. We avoid passing judgment and focus on clear, evidence-based reporting. When controversies arise, we respond calmly and present the facts.
8. How do you foster inclusivity in the newsroom?
Muneeta Aneja: I mentor my team, encourage diverse perspectives, and emphasise ethical reporting. Trust and open dialogue are central — everyone’s ideas are heard, and we work collaboratively to ensure quality and fairness.
9. How do you engage with readers and handle criticism?
Muneeta Aneja: We engage respectfully, answer concerns with data, and provide platforms for dialogue. Most readers value our research-based approach; when criticism arises we address it transparently and use it as an opportunity to improve.
10. How will you monetise The New Age while protecting editorial independence?
Muneeta Aneja: I plan to keep editorial content free. Monetisation will come through services aligned with our skills: research, PR consulting, and sponsored but clearly labelled partnerships. Editorial quality will never be compromised for revenue.
11. How do you uphold journalism ethics on sensitive topics?
Muneeta Aneja: Ethics guide every editorial decision. We avoid yellow journalism, adhere to media laws, and publish responsibly. If a story could cause harm, we handle it with utmost care and context.
12. How will you keep investigative reporting at the core?
Muneeta Aneja: Investigative work requires time and resources. I am committed to doing the difficult, sometimes costly work because journalism’s role is to hold power accountable. If threats arise, truth and diligence will guide our choices.
13. How will you innovate storytelling for younger audiences?
Muneeta Aneja: Short paragraphs, clear subheads, interactive youth debates and open platforms for startups and young voices. We’ll experiment with formats while keeping accessibility central.
14. How will you ensure international coverage is prioritized?
Muneeta Aneja: By sourcing reliable international partners, being patient with deep reporting, and fact-checking across multiple jurisdictions. We avoid haste and provide context to complex foreign affairs.
15. How will you measure success for The New Age?
Muneeta Aneja: Trust and reader engagement matter most. If readers reach out with thoughtful questions or students learn from our explainers, that is success. We’ll also track user metrics and qualitative feedback.
16. Tell us about exploring tribal rituals, archaeology and myth in Chhattisgarh.
Muneeta Aneja: I spent long days in the field documenting tribal practices, studying archaeology and speaking with elders, artisans, and royalties. My aim was to present a holistic picture — archaeology, folklore and lived realities — so outsiders can appreciate the state’s depth. If policies mention villages, journalists must go and verify implementation. I worked 16–21 hours/day to map culture, foodways, festivals and community practices for the book.
17. What unique tribal rituals did you encounter?
Muneeta Aneja: The tribes of Bastar practise animism and ancestor worship, hosting long, intricate festivals. In parts of Dakshin Bastar, youth socialise freely before marriage in dormitories to test compatibility; godna (tattooing) and traditional jewellery remain culturally central. Their cuisine — like red-ant chutney and bamboo-cooked stews — reflects deep food knowledge. There’s much more in my book.
