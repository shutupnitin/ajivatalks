Minakshi Dewan — The Final Farewell: Understanding India’s Last Rites

By Ajiva Talks
•
Culture / Non-fiction
•
— min read

Minakshi Dewan's The Final Farewell examines India’s funeral rites across major faiths and exposes the caste, gender and environmental realities tied to death work — from towers of silence to shamshan ghats and COVID-era pressures. Below is an edited conversation covering origins, findings and surprising stories from her research.

Quick facts

Book: The Final Farewell

Field: Author & researcher

Focus: Death rites, ritual specialists, caste & gender

Research, interviews, on-ground reporting

1. What inspired you to delve into the subject of death rites and rituals in India for your book, "The Final Farewell"?
Minakshi Dewan: I was inspired after I performed my papa’s antim sanskaar as a chief mourner. Certain moments intrigued me — the funeral pandit’s line, “Hum sirf mrityu se jude kaam karte hain,” and the death worker who assisted us. Months later, the pandemic overwhelmed us and disposal of the dead became a crisis. That clash — personal experience plus a public catastrophe — compelled me to document death work and how people dealt with grief during the pandemic. The book idea was born amidst that global crisis.
2. Can you provide an overview of the beliefs associated with death in India as portrayed in your book?
Minakshi Dewan: I cover five major faiths: Sikh, Zoroastrian (Parsi), Islamic, Hindu and Christian rites. Sikhs and Hindus predominantly cremate, Muslims and Christians bury, and Zoroastrians expose their dead on Towers of Silence. The book describes preparatory rites and post-cremation/burial traditions, and explores meanings behind rituals through interviews with ritual specialists, filmmakers, researchers and death workers — showing both diversity and surprising similarities.
3. You mentioned the book reveals misogyny and caste-based discrimination. How do these aspects manifest in death ceremonies?
Minakshi Dewan: Gender appears throughout — for example, mourning roles are often confined to women while many ritual tasks are male-dominated. Caste shows up sharply in death work: many tasks are caste-specific and largely fall on Dalit communities. During COVID I saw how cremation and burial workers — essential public-health actors — faced stigma, hazardous conditions and low pay. Documenting their lives was crucial; their contribution was heroic yet neglected.
4. Who are the key figures involved in managing the deceased and conducting rites across different faiths?
Minakshi Dewan: Each faith has designated specialists who guide the bereaved. Among Hindus there are specific pandits; Parsis have priests overseeing Towers of Silence; different communities rely on funeral directors, cremation workers, burial staff and death workers. I detail these roles and their responsibilities and highlight lesser-known actors like professional mourners and death workers who physically manage the deceased.
5. Your book explores economic and environmental costs associated with final rites. Can you explain these impacts?
Minakshi Dewan: There are clear environmental costs — wood for open pyre cremations contributes to deforestation and air pollution; burials can contaminate underground water and burial space is shrinking. Economic costs are significant for families who undertake elaborate rituals. Some practices, like Parsi sky burials, faced challenges when vulture populations declined due to drugs (diclofenac). The book examines these trade-offs and how tradition interacts with ecology and economy.
6. During your research, did you find any particularly poignant anecdotes you can share?
Minakshi Dewan: Research was both difficult and rewarding. I visited shamshans in Delhi and Varanasi and interviewed women priests, Parsi priests and Tirath Purohits. Many accounts of discrimination were distressing — for example, communities forced to bury loved ones near their living quarters due to exclusion. The stories of cremation and burial workers during COVID — their stamina and risk — were especially poignant and inspiring.
7. How do different faiths handle death and afterlife, and how do the rituals reflect those beliefs?
Minakshi Dewan: Rituals stem from conceptions of death and afterlife. Hindus and Sikhs cremate reflecting ideas of release and rebirth; Muslims and Christians bury aligned with beliefs about accountability and the afterlife; Parsis expose their dead in Towers of Silence tied to their theology. I unpack how beliefs shape preparatory rites, mourning periods and post-rite customs across communities.
8. The book mentions worship of bodies at the pyre and professional mourners. What is the significance and evolution of these practices?
Minakshi Dewan: Lamenting traditions exist worldwide: Rudaali in Rajasthan, Mirasans in Punjab, Oppari in Tamil Nadu. Historically such practices were social mechanisms for expressing grief and for communities to ritualize loss. Over time they’ve evolved; the book traces current avatars of these traditions and how professionalized mourning interacts with changing social norms.
9. What do you hope readers take away from "The Final Farewell"?
Minakshi Dewan: The book unravels mechanics around last rites, introduces actors involved, and analyses deeper themes — death tourism in Varanasi, the death industry, and pandemic-era shifts. It’s a book for everyone because mortality is universal. I’ve heard from readers who picked it up at airports after cremations; I hope it becomes an anchor for others seeking answers and helps start necessary conversations about death work.
10. Based on your research, what reforms are needed to address misogyny, caste discrimination and environmental impacts tied to death rituals?
Minakshi Dewan: There are no simple solutions — these issues are deeply embedded. Environmental advocates are exploring greener alternatives, but we need awareness, receptivity and systemic changes. Addressing caste and gender bias requires sustained social reform, legal protections for death workers, better pay and health safeguards, plus inclusive policies for access to funeral facilities. The book aims to spark precisely those conversations.
