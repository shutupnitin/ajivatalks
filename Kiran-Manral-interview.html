Kiran Manral — Rising 2.0: 20 More Women Who Changed India

By Ajiva Talks
•
Non-fiction / Interviews
•
— min read

Kiran Manral continues her series with Rising 2.0, adding 20 more profiles of women who shaped modern India. Below is an edited conversation about why she extended the project, how she selects subjects, her genre-hopping practice, and the stories that touched her most.

Quick facts

Book: Rising 2.0: 20 More Women Who Changed India

Role: Author & journalist

Focus: Women’s stories, research, narrative non-fiction

Also: blogger, founder of India Helps

Donate

1. Your latest book, "Rising 2.0: 20 More Women Who Changed India", highlights exceptional Indian women. What inspired you to continue the series and how did you choose the women featured?
Kiran Manral: When I made the first book (Rising — 30 Women Who Changed India), I already had a longer list that had to be trimmed to fit the word count. I realised a definitive list of thirty was impossible — many more deserved inclusion — and that led to Rising 2.0. I aimed for geographical spread, varied professions and socio-economic backgrounds; most importantly, I included women whose stories moved me or who achieved something extraordinary against the odds.
2. Among the women in Rising 2.0, which story resonated most with you personally, and why?
Kiran Manral: Kamala Das resonated strongly. A brave, iconoclastic writer who lived on her terms, she wrote in stolen pockets of time while fulfilling roles as mother and homemaker. I identified with carving out time in fragments to create. Her courage and refusal to conform continue to inspire my own attempt to reposition women in fiction as full, complex humans.
3. Your career spans advertising, journalism and books. How have these experiences influenced your writing style and subject choices?
Kiran Manral: I write because I must; the shifts helped me switch voices and genres easily. My journalism honed research skills and a fact-first approach for non-fiction; advertising taught brevity; blogging disciplined daily writing. The result is an ability to toggle between comic, dark, research-based and experimental works.
4. You transitioned from blogging to books. What motivated the shift, and how did blogging help?
Kiran Manral: Blogging was my riyaaz — daily practice that built discipline. Writing every day, even briefly, prepared me for books. The transition felt natural: the habits, the voice and the readiness were already there.
5. In "Karmic Kids" you draw on personal parenting experience. How has writing about personal life shaped your fiction and non-fiction?
Kiran Manral: Karmic Kids started as a parenting blog turned book — autofiction in a way. Fiction inevitably draws from lived experience: locations, emotions, small traumas. The craft is in camouflaging those elements. My non-fiction, however, relies on research and facts; my lived experience appears mostly in parenting books and essays.
6. You write across genres — romance, mystery, psychological thrillers. How do you choose your next genre, and do you have a favorite?
Kiran Manral: I have a low boredom threshold, so I often switch to the opposite of what I just wrote. A comic mystery might follow a dark noir; a sci-fi may follow research-based non-fiction. I work on multiple projects and pick what fits my mood that day. That keeps the work fresh.
7. "The Face at the Window" is a brooding, mysterious tale. What inspired it and how did you craft its characters and plot?
Kiran Manral: The idea began on a trip to Te Aroha at Dhanachuli near Mukteshwar: isolated cottages sparked a thought about loneliness and waiting to die. From that image came Mrs McNally — a retired Anglo-Indian teacher — and the secrets, illegitimacy and haunting that populate the mountains. The setting amplified the story’s spooky atmosphere.
8. Your India Helps volunteer work is notable. Does activism influence your writing?
Kiran Manral: I keep them in separate compartments — my volunteering and writing don’t directly influence each other, though both are important parts of my life.
9. Have you faced challenges as a female author in India, and how have you navigated them?
Kiran Manral: I haven’t felt specific challenges for being female beyond occasional dismissive readers who claim they don’t read women authors. I still feel I’m building readership and hope to reach more readers over time.
10. Looking ahead, what themes or projects are you curious to explore next?
Kiran Manral: I love experimenting. Recently I’ve veered darker in tone, and I haven’t written a funny book in a while — maybe the next one will be comic. I remain curious where mood and idea take me next.
