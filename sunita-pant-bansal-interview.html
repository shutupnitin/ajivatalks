Stories, Roots & Wonder — A Conversation with Sunita Pant Bansal

By Ajiva Talks • Author Interviews • — min read

Sunita Pant Bansal has spent four decades shaping children’s literature and writing for adults — from poems at age seven to hundreds of books, multimedia projects, and collaborations with global publishers. Her wide-ranging work brings India’s folk literature and mythology into the hands of younger readers while exploring body, mind and spirit for adult audiences.

Born in 1960 in Delhi, Sunita Pant Bansal discovered her voice early. A voracious reader and natural storyteller, she wrote her first poem at eight and regularly contributed to her school magazine, later becoming editor of the school newsletter. Over the past forty years Sunita has written articles, short stories, puzzles, board games, multimedia CD-ROMs, short films and hundreds of books for children and young adults, often focusing on folk literature and mythology. Her adult writing explores themes of body, mind and soul and has been translated into multiple languages.

Beyond authorship, Sunita’s career spans publishing, editorial leadership and media collaborations. She has headed publishing houses and served as founding editor of newspapers and magazines in India, the United States and the UK. Her creative partnerships include work with Walt Disney, Warner Bros., and Pearson Education, and she has contributed to major outlets such as The Times of India, Hindustan Times and the ABP Group.

1. When did you first realize that you wanted to be a writer?
Sunita Pant Bansal: I started writing in school — my first poem was published when I was seven. I regularly contributed to the school magazine and enjoyed holiday assignments where I made albums of stories and poems. In high school I started a news tabloid with friends. Later, while studying Nutrition in college, I became a dietician and wrote health columns for newspapers to earn pocket money. Manohar Shyam Joshi ji, for whose paper (Weekend Review, Hindustan Times) I used to write, encouraged me to write on other subjects — and that’s when I realised writing was my real passion.
2. You’ve written more than a thousand books for children. How did that begin?
Sunita Pant Bansal: I have written over a thousand books for children of all ages. Writing for children is not easy, but Rosalind Wilson, editor of Target (a children’s magazine of the India Today group), encouraged me. We met by chance and, after learning I was a writer, she suggested I try children’s literature and essentially gave me a tutorial on how to write for young readers. That’s how I entered the field. It’s challenging but incredibly gratifying when parents share photos of their children reading my books.
3. Many bookstores say they struggle because of Amazon and Flipkart. What’s your take?
Sunita Pant Bansal: Bookstores do face competition from online portals and publishers who sell directly. They often stock books from publishers who offer credit deals, better discounts and make efforts to promote titles. Brick-and-mortar shops have to find ways to survive — for example, by offering curated selections or events that create community value.
4. What major changes have you seen in the industry over your forty years?
Sunita Pant Bansal: The number of independent bookstores and public libraries has reduced, but I also see reading corners in cafes and a growing interest among parents in buying quality books for children. Online bookselling and self-publishing have increased, bringing more writers and more variety of books. Overall, there are more readers and more choices than before.
5. How many unpublished or half-finished books do you have?
Sunita Pant Bansal: Fortunately, I don’t have unpublished or half-finished books at the moment, though I have many ideas that I plan to develop in the coming years.
6. Which part of Everyday Gita: 365 Days of Wisdom is your personal favorite?
Sunita Pant Bansal: The entire process of writing Everyday Gita is special. I spent a major part of the writing in Gethia, Uttarakhand. Each chapter holds its own meaning, so it’s difficult to pick a single favourite.
7. Ajitabha Bose said that without a good budget you can’t reach your audience. Do you agree?
Sunita Pant Bansal: I don’t fully agree. A book has its own destiny and often reaches where it must eventually. A budget can speed exposure, but without substance a book won’t endure. Good promotion helps, but lasting impact comes from the book itself.
