Bina Nayak — Goagram, Influence & Finding Timelessness in Goa

By Ajiva Talks
•
Author Interview / Culture
•
— min read

Bina Nayak — celebrated graphic designer turned novelist — explores the collision of influencer culture and local life in her new novel Goagram. We talk to her about Casa Coutinho, Goa beyond the postcards, and why digital fame must be tempered by craft and care.

Quick facts

Works: Starfish Pickle; Goagram

Field: Graphic designer (28+ years), author

From / Lives: Born Bombay, lives in Goa

Design, writing, Goa, storytelling

Donate

1. Can you tell us about your inspiration behind writing "Goagram"? What prompted you to explore the world of social media influencing and its impacts?
Bina Nayak: I used to live in Parra, Goa, near a road that was briefly featured in the Bollywood film Dear Zindagi. After the film became a hit we started getting tourists asking for directions to that road. After COVID those trickles became a flood and influencers started posing and creating reels there — sometimes lying down on the tar to get the right shot. That was my jogging track and I'd dodge them. The image of a coconut falling on their heads became the opening paragraph of Goagram.

My years in advertising and media fuelled the curiosity: I saw influencers replacing models and providing economical advertising for small businesses. That professional lens plus local observation pushed me to explore the phenomenon — its upside and its costs.
2. The protagonist, Madhur Chopra (Maddie), undergoes a big transformation. What aspects of her character did you find most compelling to explore?
Bina Nayak: Maddie’s shift from ‘taker’ to ‘giver’ — from a tourist mindset to someone integrated with local life — was most satisfying. She begins selfishly, treating Goa as something to be consumed. Living with locals like Mrs Coutinho, learning Konkani, absorbing customs and rhythms — that evolution grounded her and was rewarding to write.
3. "Goagram" looks at digital fame vs. analog authenticity. Can you elaborate on this theme?
Bina Nayak: Digital fame is fleeting; analog authenticity endures. The novel shows how social media fame brings trolling, anxiety and insecurity, while creating a real craft — e.g., designing a dress that employs people and lasts decades — gives deeper satisfaction. I wanted readers to weigh the short-term glitter against long-term value.
4. Why Goa as the setting — how does it shape the story?
Bina Nayak: I’m a Goan and live here. Goa isn’t just beaches and parties; it has houses, temples, schools, doctors, farmers. I wanted to depict the real Goa beyond the tourist belt so visitors see the people who live here. The story asks readers to be sensitive to local culture — not treat heritage as a backdrop for content creation.
5. Casa Coutinho feels like a character. What does it mean in Maddie's journey?
Bina Nayak: Many Goan houses are centuries old; they acquire personality. Casa Coutinho, shaped by Mrs Coutinho, is fraying but solid — a metaphor for values and a righteous older generation. For Maddie, who’s used to ephemeral modern constructions, the mansion represents timelessness and legacy. It inspires her to aim for something enduring, not a 15-second fame spike.
6. The friendship between Maddie and her landlady is central. What message did you want to convey?
Bina Nayak: We have much to learn from those with experience. Interacting across generations grows empathy and tolerance. Too often we stay within our tribes; meeting different people helps us evolve. Maddie’s friendship with Mrs Coutinho shows mutual learning and the richness that comes from crossing boundaries.
7. What commentary do you offer on influencer culture and its impacts?
Bina Nayak: I advocate for caution and care. Influencing is here to stay and can be a gateway to bigger things, but the industry needs guidelines and protections. Influencers and communities must be safer, and creators should be mindful of the real-world consequences of their content.
8. Without spoiling, can you hint at the pivotal moment that shatters Maddie's digital dreams?
Bina Nayak: That would be a spoiler, but the novel’s blurb hints: a place in the sun comes at a cost. To grow, Maddie must unlearn parts of herself and reinvent. Sometimes growth requires losing a version of you that no longer serves.
9. Why the title "Goagram" — what does it signify?
Bina Nayak: It’s simple: a play on Goa + Instagram. 'Gram' also means village — Goa is in many ways a village. For visitors, Goa is addictive like social media: once you check in, it’s hard to check out. The title captures that lure and the village reality beneath the feed.
10. The ending forces Maddie to choose between fame and anonymity. What do you hope readers take away from that choice?
Bina Nayak: Leave your comfort zone and take a leap of faith. Create things of lasting value — a timeless dress, a house that stands for centuries — not a meme or a moment. Aim to be classic, not a one-meme wonder.
